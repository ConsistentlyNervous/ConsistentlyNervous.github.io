Masked

Welcome to the Venetian Carnival!

Masked is a detective visual novel game set during a traditional Venetian Carnival. The Great Noble Bauta has been murdered and you've been contracted to find his killer! The suspects are shallow and absolutely judge a book by its cover. Use a selection of masks to appease them and gather all the information you can before making your final verdict.

My Design Process - Early Sketches

For this project, I was really interested in how visually obstructing information from the player can create curiosity and interest to play the same path a different way, in order to fill in the blanks. I got really stuck on the idea of masks and using an “equipped mask” to portray a specific path the character will take.

Initial Sketch

After this initial mockup, I wanted to play with different ways the information could be visually hidden to the player and the obtrusiveness of it all. With this I wanted to start taking into account balancing curiosity vs frustration.

Top - Blocked dialogue paths

Bottom - Blocked question buttons

Top - Hidden info on personal cards, used at a later stage

Bottom - Hidden information in a personal notebook

Top - NPC dialogue blocked out

Bottom - NPC visuals and dialogue blocked out

With these mockups in front of me I was able to start looking more at how I can balance curiosity and frustration. I wasn't the biggest fan of how gameplay would feel for the later 4, since they really amp up the amount of cognitive load needed to interact with the system. Forcing the player to remember too much information as they flip through screens just feels a bit cruel.

With the first two the cognitive load isn't as arduous intrinsically. Of course they require the player to remember information but it's complete information, which makes it so much easier on the player. This load could even be lightened further by making the really important information closer to the end of the interaction so its fresher.

I chose to move forward with my second design, that hides dialogue choice buttons based on what mask the player has on. I felt that this would be the most realistic for our team to realistically develop and polish over a month and would be more of a design challenge for me.

Design Challenges

There were many design challenges faced during the time I spent working on this project. However, I want to focus on just 2 of those

1. Action Button Naming

2. Mask Overlay Sizing

Starting with the action buttons, I wanted the interface to feature 3 consistent buttons with both iconography and text to more wholly depict the dialogue path. I felt the buttons needed to be consistent so that players could easily know what was available/hidden from them, so they can better plan their next mask choice.

First Iteration Button Names - Question, Victim, and Motive

Through some initial playtests with these button names revealed that “Question” was incredibly confusing to players needed to be changed. I touched base with our narrative member to explore what tone of dialogue they wrote for this button and brainstormed some ideas with them. We landed on “Introduction” with a new handshake icon, and this change was received really well by players!

Final Iteration Button Names - Introduction, Victim, and Motive

The next hurdle this project had to face was the mask overlay and making sure it wasn't too distracting or taking up too much screen real estate. Luckily since this game is largely text based we had more wiggle room than a movement/speed based game. One thing that was really helpful was putting UI over top of the mask outline, this helped to keep the important information visible to players and separate elements into “what the player character sees” and “what options I have as a player.”

Retrospective

The only thing I would change about this final design is the color scheme of the buttons. We sadly didn't have time to make new art to better match the color scheme our artist decided to go with for the characters. But other than that this was an incredibly fun project to work on and I'm really glad I got to work through a lot of these design challenges with a great team.
